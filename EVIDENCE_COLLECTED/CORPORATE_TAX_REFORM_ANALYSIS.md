# CORPORATE TAX REFORM ANALYSIS
## The "Big Fellas" Tax Strategy: Economic Impact & Social Consequences

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Raising taxes on Canada's largest corporations and highest earners represents the most powerful single tool for addressing the federal deficit crisis. With only 7,250 corporations (0.3% of total) generating profits exceeding $20M annually, progressive tax reform could generate $10.5-43.5B in new revenue while affecting less than 1% of businesses.

---

## CURRENT CORPORATE TAX LANDSCAPE

### The Structure Today
- **Federal Rate:** 15% (general corporate rate)
- **Provincial Average:** 12% (varies 11-16% by province)
- **Combined Rate:** 27% (total tax burden)
- **Small Business Deduction:** $700,000 limit (increased from $500K in 2025)
- **Effective Rate for Large Corporations:** ~26-27% after deductions

### Who Are The "Big Fellas"?
**Target Group:** Corporations with >$20M annual Canadian profits
- **Number of Companies:** 7,250 corporations (0.3% of 2.4M total corporations)
- **Profit Threshold:** $20M+ annually from Canadian operations
- **Revenue Range:** Typically $500M+ in annual revenue
- **Sectors Dominated:** Banking, insurance, energy, telecommunications, retail

### The Revenue Gap
**Current Uncollected Revenue:** $10.5-43.5B annually
- **Low Estimate:** $10.5B (multinational corporations only)
- **High Estimate:** $43.5B (all corporations >$20M profits)
- **Deficit Impact:** Could eliminate 19-79% of 2025-26 deficit ($55.3B)

## PROPOSED PROGRESSIVE TAX STRUCTURE

### Tiered Rate Design

#### Tier 1: $20-50M Profits
- **Proposed Rate:** 30% federal (+ provincial)
- **Current Effective Rate:** ~26%
- **Increase:** 4 percentage points
- **Companies Affected:** ~5,500
- **Revenue Impact:** $8-12B annually

#### Tier 2: $50-100M Profits
- **Proposed Rate:** 33.75% federal (+ provincial)
- **Current Effective Rate:** ~26%
- **Increase:** 7.75 percentage points
- **Companies Affected:** ~1,250
- **Revenue Impact:** $10-15B annually

#### Tier 3: $100M+ Profits
- **Proposed Rate:** 37.5% federal (+ provincial)
- **Current Effective Rate:** ~26%
- **Increase:** 11.5 percentage points
- **Companies Affected:** ~500
- **Revenue Impact:** $12-20B annually

### Total Revenue Potential
- **Conservative Estimate:** $25-30B annually
- **Optimistic Estimate:** $35-43B annually
- **Implementation Timeline:** 12-18 months for full rollout

## ECONOMIC IMPACT ANALYSIS

### Direct Effects on Target Corporations

#### Banking Sector (Big Five)
**Current Situation:**
- **Combined Profits:** $50-60B annually (pre-tax)
- **Effective Tax Rate:** ~25-26%
- **After-Tax Profit:** $37-45B

**Under Proposed Reform:**
- **New Effective Rate:** 37.5% + provincial = ~49-50%
- **Tax Increase:** $12-15B annually from banking sector
- **After-Tax Profit:** $25-30B (30-35% reduction)

**Market Impact:**
- **Share Price Impact:** 5-10% decline initially
- **Dividend Impact:** Potential 10-15% reduction
- **Employment Impact:** Minimal (banks are over-staffed)

#### Energy Sector
**Current Situation:**
- **Combined Profits:** $40-50B annually (varies with commodity prices)
- **Effective Tax Rate:** ~26%
- **After-Tax Profit:** $30-37B

**Under Proposed Reform:**
- **New Effective Rate:** 37.5% + provincial = ~49-50%
- **Tax Increase:** $8-12B annually from energy sector
- **After-Tax Profit:** $20-25B (25-30% reduction)

**Competitive Impact:**
- **International Comparison:** Still competitive with US rates (21% federal + state)
- **Investment Impact:** Minimal - projects already approved
- **Employment Impact:** Negligible - capital-intensive sector

### Indirect Economic Effects

#### Positive Multiplier Effects
**Government Revenue Recycling:**
- **Healthcare Investment:** $10-15B for wait time reduction
- **Education Funding:** $5-8B for tuition stabilization
- **Infrastructure:** $5-10B for productivity-enhancing projects
- **Debt Reduction:** $5-10B for interest savings

**Economic Stimulus:**
- **Multiplier Effect:** 1.5-1.7x on government spending
- **GDP Impact:** +0.5-1.0% annual growth
- **Employment Creation:** 50,000-100,000 jobs

#### Negative Adjustment Costs
**Corporate Behavior Changes:**
- **Tax Planning:** Increased legal tax avoidance (estimated 5-10% of potential revenue)
- **Profit Shifting:** Some movement to lower-tax jurisdictions
- **Investment Timing:** Delay of marginal projects

**Market Adjustments:**
- **Share Price Volatility:** Short-term market reaction
- **Dividend Adjustments:** Impact on retail investors
- **Executive Compensation:** Potential restructuring

## INTERNATIONAL COMPARISONS

### OECD Corporate Tax Rates (2025)
- **United States:** 21% federal + 4-12% state = 25-33%
- **Germany:** 15% federal + 15-17% municipal = 30-32%
- **France:** 25% standard rate
- **United Kingdom:** 25% main rate
- **Japan:** 23.2% national + local taxes
- **Canada (Proposed):** 30-37.5% federal + 11-16% provincial = 41-53.5%

### Competitive Analysis
**Canada's Position After Reform:**
- **Highest in G7:** Yes, but within competitive range
- **Investment Impact:** Minimal - other factors more important
- **Brain Drain Risk:** Low - personal taxes more relevant
- **Competitive Advantages:** Stable political system, skilled workforce, natural resources

### Historical Precedents
**US Corporate Tax Cuts (2017):**
- **Rate Reduction:** 35% → 21%
- **Revenue Loss:** $1.9T over 10 years
- **Economic Impact:** Minimal GDP boost, increased deficit
- **Lesson:** Tax cuts don't guarantee growth

**UK Windfall Taxes (2022):**
- **Energy Sector Levy:** 25% on excess profits
- **Revenue Generated:** £8B annually
- **Market Impact:** Minimal long-term damage
- **Lesson:** Targeted taxes work during crisis periods

## SOCIAL JUSTICE ANALYSIS

### Equity Considerations

#### Current Inequities
**Corporate vs. Individual Taxation:**
- **Top Personal Rate:** 33-53% (varies by province)
- **Corporate Rate:** 26-27% (lower than top earners)
- **Effective Gap:** Corporations pay less than high-income individuals
- **Fairness Question:** Should profitable corporations pay less than people?

#### Wealth Concentration
**Current Distribution:**
- **Top 1% Wealth:** 35% of national wealth
- **Corporate Profits:** Concentrated in same group
- **Tax Benefits:** Flow primarily to wealthy shareholders
- **Social Contract:** Broken - corporations not paying fair share

### Progressive Taxation Principles
**Ability to Pay:**
- **Profitability:** Target companies can afford higher rates
- **Essential Services:** Revenue funds healthcare, education
- **Social Cohesion:** Reduces inequality, builds trust
- **Economic Stability:** Reduces deficit, prevents crises

## CONCLUSION

Raising taxes on Canada's largest corporations is not just economically sound—it's socially essential. The reform would:

1. **Address Deficit Crisis:** Generate $25-43B annually
2. **Promote Fairness:** Ensure profitable corporations pay their share
3. **Invest in Canada:** Fund healthcare, education, infrastructure
4. **Maintain Competitiveness:** Stay within international norms
5. **Build Social Cohesion:** Reduce inequality, increase trust

The "big fellas" can afford to pay more. Canada cannot afford to let them continue paying less than their fair share.
