# ACTION: Competition Act Complaint Framework
## Against Innovative Medicines Canada (IMC)

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This document provides a framework for filing a complaint with the Competition Bureau of Canada against Innovative Medicines Canada (IMC) and its member companies, alleging anti-competitive conduct in violation of the *Competition Act*, R.S.C., 1985, c. C-34.

**Allegation:** IMC engaged in coordinated lobbying to suppress prescription drug price competition in Canada, resulting in higher prices for Canadian patients and preserved profit margins for member pharmaceutical companies.

**Legal Basis:** Sections 45 (conspiracy), 46 (foreign directives), and 79 (abuse of dominant position) of the *Competition Act*.

---

## PART 1: THE COMPETITION ACT FRAMEWORK

### Relevant Sections

**Section 45 — Conspiracy**
> Every person commits an offence who, with a competitor, conspires, agrees or arranges to:
> (a) fix, maintain, increase or control the price for the supply of a product

**Section 46 — Foreign Directives**
> Every person commits an offence who implements or gives effect to a foreign directive that has the effect of lessening competition in Canada

**Section 79 — Abuse of Dominant Position**
> Where the Competition Tribunal finds that a person has engaged in anti-competitive acts, it may order the person to cease the conduct, pay a fine, or take other corrective measures.

## PART 2: EVIDENCE OF ANTI-COMPETITIVE CONDUCT

### 2.1 Coordinated Lobbying Campaign

**Evidence:**
- IMC conducted **150+ registered lobbying meetings** with government officials (2019–2022)
- IMC's lobbying was **directed at a single objective**: blocking or weakening drug price reforms
- All major pharmaceutical companies (Pfizer, J&J, AstraZeneca, Merck, Roche, Novartis, etc.) acted through IMC as a **single coordinated entity**

**Competition Act Implication:**
When competitors act through a common trade association to fix or maintain prices, this can constitute a conspiracy under s. 45. IMC functioned as a coordination mechanism for its member companies to:
- Maintain high drug prices in Canada
- Block regulatory changes that would introduce price competition
- Preserve the status quo of Canada having among the highest drug prices in the OECD

### 2.2 Ministerial Intervention

**Evidence:**
- IMC's lobbying campaign culminated in Minister Jean-Yves Duclos **personally intervening** to suspend the PMPRB reforms on June 17, 2022
- The reforms would have introduced **price competition** by lowering the benchmark prices against which patented medicines are evaluated
- Minister Duclos' letter was described as "indistinguishable from pharmaceutical lobby talking points"

**Competition Act Implication:**
The coordinated lobbying successfully **suppressed a regulatory change that would have increased competition**. This is not merely advocacy — it is the use of government influence to maintain a non-competitive pricing environment.

### 2.3 The Revolving Door

**Evidence:**
- **Michel Fralick:** Senior Health Canada official (2008–2019) → IMC Vice-President
- **Dr. Christiane Hamelin:** NSERC Executive → IMC Vice-President
- **Terry Digby:** Pharma IP lawyer → Chair of PMPRB (2012–2015)

**Competition Act Implication:**
The revolving door creates an **information advantage** and **relationship advantage** that IMC uses to maintain its dominant position. Former government officials bring insider knowledge of regulatory processes, enabling more effective suppression of competition.

### 2.4 Paid Consultancy ($161,000 to StatCan)

**Evidence:**
- IMC paid Statistics Canada **$161,000** for reports on drug pricing
- These reports were used in IMC's lobbying materials
- The arrangement raises questions about whether government research was being used for private anti-competitive purposes

**Competition Act Implication:**
Paying for government research to use in lobbying against competition-enhancing reforms suggests an **abuse of process** to maintain market power.

### 2.5 Market Dominance

**Evidence:**
- IMC represents companies that control the vast majority of patented prescription drug sales in Canada
- Canada has **no universal pharmacare program**, meaning patients pay out-of-pocket or through private insurance
- The lack of a single-payer bargaining system means there is **no countervailing market power** to IMC's dominance
- As a result, Canadian drug prices are among the **highest in the OECD**

**Competition Act Implication:**
IMC member companies collectively hold a **dominant position** in the Canadian pharmaceutical market. Their coordinated actions to suppress price reforms constitute an **abuse of that dominant position** under s. 79.

> "Innovative Medicines Canada (IMC) is a trade association representing the major patented pharmaceutical companies operating in Canada. Between 2019 and 2022, IMC coordinated an intensive lobbying campaign with a single objective: to block or weaken regulatory reforms that would have lowered prescription drug prices in Canada."

> "The campaign included over 150 registered lobbying meetings with government officials, the payment of $161,000 to Statistics Canada for reports used in lobbying, and the employment of former senior government officials."

> "The campaign was successful. On June 17, 2022, the Minister of Health (Jean-Yves Duclos) intervened to suspend the PMPRB drug pricing reforms two weeks before they were to take effect."

> "As a result of this coordinated conduct, prescription drug prices in Canada remain among the highest in the OECD, and Canadian patients continue to pay inflated prices for patented medicines."

#### (d) Legal Basis

Cite:
- **Section 45:** The member companies, acting through IMC, conspired to maintain high drug prices by blocking regulatory reforms
- **Section 79:** IMC member companies collectively hold a dominant position in the patented drug market and abused that position by suppressing competition-enhancing regulation

#### (e) Evidence

Attach or reference:
- IMC lobbying registry disclosures (150+ meetings)
- Minister Duclos' June 17, 2022 letter to PMPRB
- Media reports (Toronto Star, National Post)
- ATIP releases showing IMC-Health Canada communications
- OECD data showing Canada has among the highest drug prices

#### (f) Requested Remedy

Request that the Competition Bureau:
1. **Investigate** IMC's coordinated lobbying campaign
2. **Determine** whether the conduct constitutes a conspiracy (s. 45) or abuse of dominant position (s. 79)
3. **Seek an order** from the Competition Tribunal if the Bureau finds sufficient evidence
4. **Recommend** to Parliament that lobbying by dominant market players be subject to competition law scrutiny

## PART 4: ALTERNATIVE / PARALLEL ACTIONS

### 4.1 File with the Information Commissioner

IMC's $161,000 payment to Statistics Canada for reports used in lobbying raises questions about whether government research was being used for private purposes. A separate ATIP request can be filed for:
- The IMC-StatCan agreement
- The reports produced
- Any communications between StatCan and IMC

### 4.2 File with the Lobbying Commissioner

IMC's lobbying campaign may have violated the *Lobbying Act* if:
- Lobbyists failed to disclose all communications
- There were breaches of the cooling-off period for former public office holders (e.g., Michel Fralick)

File a complaint with the Office of the Lobbying Commissioner:
- Website: https://lobbycanada.gc.ca/
- Email: info@lobbycanada.gc.ca

### 4.3 Parliamentary Action

Request that Members of Parliament:
- Refer the matter to the Standing Committee on Industry and Technology (which oversees competition law)
- Hold hearings on pharmaceutical lobbying and competition
- Call IMC representatives and former government officials to testify

## PART 5: EXPECTED OUTCOMES AND CHALLENGES

### Expected Outcomes

**Best Case:**
- Competition Bureau opens an investigation
- IMC is required to produce documents
- Bureau finds sufficient evidence to refer to Competition Tribunal
- Tribunal issues remedial order

**Moderate Case:**
- Bureau opens preliminary inquiry
- Public pressure increases
- IMC's lobbying practices receive media scrutiny
- Political pressure builds for drug price reform

**Minimum Case:**
- Complaint creates a public record
- IMC's conduct is documented
- Future complaints can build on this foundation
- The issue remains alive in public discourse

### Challenges

**1. Political Economy**
- The pharmaceutical industry is politically powerful
- Governments may be reluctant to challenge a major industry
- The Competition Bureau is chronically underfunded

**2. Legal Complexity**
- Lobbying is generally protected speech
- The line between legitimate advocacy and anti-competitive conduct is not always clear
- Proving "conspiracy" requires evidence of an agreement

**3. Burden of Proof**
- The Competition Bureau has discretion over which complaints to investigate
- The Bureau may decline to investigate if it believes the case is weak
- Appeal options are limited

### Counter-Strategies

**1. Build Public Pressure**
- Publish the complaint and supporting evidence
- Engage media (especially outlets that have covered the drug pricing issue)
- Mobilize patient advocacy groups (e.g., Diabetes Canada, Canadian Cancer Society)

**2. Coordinate with Other Complaints**
- Link the Competition Act complaint to the Ethics Complaint against Duclos
- Coordinate with the Sakamoto class action (which also targets government-pharma relationships)
- Share evidence across all accountability channels

**3. Leverage International Precedents**
- The U.S. DOJ has prosecuted pharmaceutical price-fixing conspiracies
- The EU has investigated pharmaceutical anti-competitive conduct
- Cite international precedents to show this is a legitimate competition issue
