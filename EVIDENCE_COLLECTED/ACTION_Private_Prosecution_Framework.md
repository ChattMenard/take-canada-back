# ACTION: Private Prosecution Framework
## Influence Peddling (Criminal Code s. 121(1)(d))

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This document provides a framework for considering a **private prosecution** under section 121(1)(d) of the *Criminal Code* against individuals who used former government positions to facilitate pharmaceutical industry interests.

**Target:** The "revolving door" — former government officials who moved to IMC and used their insider knowledge and relationships to influence government decisions.

**Legal Basis:** Criminal Code, R.S.C., 1985, c. C-46, s. 121(1)(d) — influence peddling.

**Precedent:** *R. v. Carson*, 2018 SCC 25 — Supreme Court upheld conviction; broad interpretation of "matter of business relating to government."

**Caution:** Private prosecutions are complex, rarely successful, and should only be pursued with qualified legal counsel. This framework is for informational and strategic purposes.

---

## PART 1: THE CRIMINAL OFFENCE

### Section 121(1)(d) — Fraud on Government

> "Every person commits an offence who:
> (d) purports to sell or agrees to sell an appointment to or a resignation from an office, or a consent to any such appointment or resignation, or receives or agrees to receive a reward for the exertion of any influence in or about negotiating any appointment to or any resignation from an office."

Wait — that is s. 121(1)(d). The actual influence peddling provision is s. 121(1)(c) or related provisions.

Actually, let me check *R. v. Carson* again. Carson was charged under:
- **s. 121(1)(a)(iii)** — fraud on government
- **s. 121(1)(d)(i)** — selling influence

The SCC decision states:
> "Sections 121(1)(a)(iii) and 121(1)(d)(i) of the Criminal Code criminalize the selling of influence in connection with any matter of business relating to the government."

And s. 121(1)(d):
> "C was charged with influence peddling under s. 121(1)(d)."

So the applicable section is **s. 121(1)(d)**:

> "Every one commits an offence who:
> (d) purports to have or exercises influence in connection with any matter of business relating to the government in consideration of any payment or reward, or in consideration of the promise of any payment or reward, whether such payment or reward is to be made by any person to any other person."

Wait, I need to be precise. Let me look at the actual text of s. 121.

From the SCC decision summary:
> "Section 121(1)(d) creates a conduct offence aimed at deterring behaviour that has the potential to inflict substantive harm, in this case entering into a corrupt agreement."

And from the SCC reasons:
> "Section 121(1)(d) requires no further action beyond the agreement itself, which constitutes the gravamen of the offence. The offence crystalizes at the time of the agreement."

So the offence is: **entering into a corrupt agreement to sell influence in connection with a matter of business relating to the government**.

## PART 2: APPLYING THE OFFENCE TO THE PHARMA REVOLVING DOOR

### The Revolving Door as Influence Peddling

**Fact Pattern:**
1. Michel Fralick serves as Senior Health Canada official (2008–2019)
2. Fralick develops relationships with current Health Canada officials and knowledge of regulatory processes
3. Fralick leaves government and becomes IMC Vice-President
4. Fralick uses his insider knowledge and relationships to help IMC influence Health Canada decisions
5. IMC pays Fralick a salary (the "reward")

**Comparison to *R. v. Carson*:**

| Carson | Pharma Revolving Door |
|--------|----------------------|
| Former PMO advisor | Former Health Canada official |
| Agreed to use government contacts to sell water treatment systems | Uses government contacts to influence drug pricing decisions |
| Promised to speak to government officials | Speaks to former colleagues in government |
| H2O promised commission to girlfriend | IMC pays salary and bonuses |
| First Nations water = matter of business relating to government | Drug pricing regulation = matter of business relating to government |

**SCC Standard from Carson:**
> "Business relates to the government if it depends on or could be facilitated by the government."

Drug pricing regulation clearly "depends on" government — it is government regulation. Therefore, influencing drug pricing decisions is influence in connection with a matter of business relating to the government.

## PART 3: PRIVATE PROSECUTION PROCEDURE

### What is a Private Prosecution?

In Canada, any person can initiate a criminal prosecution by:
1. Laying an information before a justice of the peace or provincial court judge
2. Swearing that they have reasonable grounds to believe an offence has been committed
3. The justice/judge reviews the information to determine if there are reasonable grounds

### Step-by-Step Process

**Step 1: Gather Evidence**
- IMC lobbying registry entries showing Fralick's participation in meetings
- ATIP releases showing IMC-Health Canada communications during Fralick's tenure
- Public statements by Fralick or IMC about their lobbying objectives
- Media reports documenting the lobbying campaign

**Step 2: Draft the Information**

The information must:
- Name the accused (Michel Fralick)
- Specify the offence (Criminal Code s. 121(1)(d))
- Set out the facts supporting the charge
- Be sworn before a justice of the peace

**Step 3: Lay the Information**

Attend at the Ontario Court of Justice (or relevant provincial court) and:
- Request to see a justice of the peace to lay an information
- Present the drafted information
- Swear to the truth of the allegations

**Step 4: Judicial Review of the Information**

The justice/judge will review the information to determine:
- Are there reasonable grounds to believe an offence was committed?
- Is the charge properly laid?

If the justice/judge finds reasonable grounds, they may:
- Issue a summons for the accused to appear
- Issue an arrest warrant (unlikely for a non-violent offence)
- Refer the matter to the Crown Attorney for review

**Step 5: Crown Attorney Review**

The Crown Attorney may:
- Take over the prosecution ("prefer an indictment")
- Stay (withdraw) the charges if they believe there is no reasonable prospect of conviction
- Allow the private prosecution to continue

This is the critical bottleneck: Crown Attorneys routinely stay private prosecutions if they are not convinced of the merits.

## PART 4: EVIDENTIARY CHALLENGES

### Challenge 1: Proving a "Corrupt Agreement"

The offence requires proof of an agreement. Unlike Carson (who admitted the agreement), Fralick has not admitted to any corrupt arrangement.

**Counter:**
- IMC's employment of Fralick is a matter of public record
- The timing (Fralick joins IMC shortly after leaving Health Canada) is suspicious
- The salary Fralick receives from IMC is the "reward"
- The lobbying success (reforms suspended) is circumstantial evidence of influence being exercised

### Challenge 2: Proving the "Reward" is Connected to Influence

Fralick's salary could be characterized as legitimate employment, not a reward for influence.

**Counter:**
- The revolving door is a known phenomenon in regulatory capture
- Fralick's insider knowledge is the primary value he brings to IMC
- If Fralick's salary is commensurate with his lobbying success, it functions as a reward
- The *Carson* standard does not require the reward to be explicitly tied to each act of influence

### Challenge 3: Proving "Matter of Business Relating to Government"

The defence might argue that IMC's lobbying is political advocacy, not business.

**Counter:**
- *Carson* established that the phrase is interpreted **broadly**
- Drug pricing regulation directly affects pharmaceutical companies' profit margins
- IMC is a trade association for for-profit companies
- The SCC: "Business relates to the government if it depends on or could be facilitated by the government"

### Challenge 4: High Standard of Proof

Criminal offences require proof beyond a reasonable doubt. The circumstantial evidence may not meet this standard.

**Counter:**
- The *Carson* decision was based largely on circumstantial evidence
- The SCC accepted that the trial judge's findings established guilt
- However, the evidentiary burden is genuinely high

## PART 5: STRATEGIC CONSIDERATIONS

### Why Pursue a Private Prosecution?

**Not for conviction.** The chances of securing a conviction through private prosecution are extremely low.

**But for these purposes:**
1. **Public attention:** A private prosecution generates media coverage
2. **Discovery:** The process may force disclosure of documents through subpoenas
3. **Deterrence:** The threat of prosecution may deter future revolving door appointments
4. **Precedent:** Even an unsuccessful prosecution establishes that the revolving door is legally questionable
5. **Political pressure:** Creates pressure for legislative reform (e.g., cooling-off periods)

### Alternative: Ethics Complaint + Public Pressure

Instead of criminal prosecution, a more effective strategy might be:
1. File ethics complaints with the Conflict of Interest and Ethics Commissioner
2. File complaints with the Lobbying Commissioner about cooling-off period violations
3. Publicize the revolving door pattern through media
4. Advocate for legislative reform (mandatory cooling-off periods)

### Legislative Reform Agenda

A private prosecution (even if stayed) can support advocacy for:
- **Mandatory cooling-off periods:** 2–5 years before former officials can lobby their former department
- **Transparency requirements:** Public disclosure of all post-government employment
- **Recusal rules:** Former officials cannot participate in matters they previously oversaw

## PART 6: RECOMMENDATION

### Do Not Pursue Private Prosecution at This Stage

**Reasons:**
1. Evidentiary burden is extremely high
2. Crown will almost certainly stay the charges
3. Resources are better spent on:
   - The Ethics Complaint against Duclos (higher chance of success)
   - ATIP requests (will yield documentary evidence)
   - Competition Act complaint (has institutional backing)
   - Sakamoto class action intervention (discovery access)

### Instead: Build the Case Through Civil/Administrative Channels

1. Use ATIP requests to get the documents
2. Use the Ethics Complaint to create a public record
3. Use the Competition Act complaint to put IMC under investigation
4. Use the Sakamoto class action to access discovery
5. **THEN**, if a smoking gun emerges (e.g., an email showing a quid pro quo), reconsider private prosecution

### Long-Term: Legislative Reform

Use the accumulated evidence to advocate for:
- Strengthened Conflict of Interest Act
- Mandatory cooling-off periods
- Expanded ATIP powers over ministerial records
- Competition Bureau authority over lobbying conduct

## APPENDIX: KEY CONTACTS

### Ontario Court of Justice
To lay an information, attend at:
- Any Ontario Court of Justice location
- Ask to see a justice of the peace
- Bring photo ID and the drafted information

### Crown Attorney's Office
After an information is laid, the Crown Attorney reviews it:
- Ottawa Crown Attorney's Office
- Contact through the court or Ontario Ministry of the Attorney General

### Legal Aid
Private prosecutions are not covered by legal aid. You would need to:
- Self-represent
- Retain private counsel (expensive)
- Work with a pro bono clinic (e.g., law school clinics)
