### SECTION OF THE ACT ALLEGEDLY CONTRAVENED

**Section 9** of the *Conflict of Interest Act*, R.S.C., 2006, c. 9, s. 2:

> "No public office holder shall use his or her position as a public office holder to seek to influence a decision of another person so as to further the private interests of the public office holder, a member of his or her family or to improperly further another person's private interests."

---

### BACKGROUND AND SUMMARY

This complaint alleges that the Honourable Jean-Yves Duclos, while serving as Minister of Health, used his position to influence a decision of the Patented Medicine Prices Review Board (PMPRB) so as to improperly further the private interests of Innovative Medicines Canada (IMC) — the trade association representing Pfizer, Johnson & Johnson, AstraZeneca, Merck, and other major pharmaceutical companies.

This complaint is supported by publicly available government records, Access to Information (ATIP) releases, investigative journalism, and parliamentary testimony.

---

### STATEMENT OF FACTS

#### 1. The PMPRB Drug Pricing Reforms

In 2019, the Government of Canada enacted amendments to the *Patented Medicines Regulations* to lower prescription drug prices, which were among the highest in the world. The reforms were set to take effect on July 1, 2022.

The reforms would have:
- Required pharmaceutical companies to report actual net prices (after rebates and discounts)
- Set new price thresholds based on international benchmarks
- Expanded the basket of comparator countries to include lower-priced jurisdictions

#### 2. IMC's Intensive Lobbying Campaign

Innovative Medicines Canada (IMC) launched an intensive lobbying campaign against the reforms:
- **150+ meetings** with government officials between 2019–2022 (disclosed in IMC's public lobbying registry)
- **$161,000 payment** to Statistics Canada for reports used in lobbying materials
- Hired former government officials, creating a "revolving door":
  - **Michel Fralick:** Senior Health Canada official (2008–2019) → IMC Vice-President
  - **Dr. Christiane Hamelin:** NSERC Executive → IMC VP
  - **Terry Digby:** Pharma IP lawyer → Chair of PMPRB (2012–2015)

#### 3. Minister Duclos' Intervention

On **June 17, 2022** — two weeks before the reforms were to take effect — Minister Duclos wrote to the PMPRB Chair directing the Board to:
- Suspend the new price benchmarks
- Delay implementation of the regulatory amendments
- Revert to the pre-reform pricing methodology

#### 4. The Letter's Content

The letter's language was described by independent analysts as **"indistinguishable from pharmaceutical lobby talking points."**

Key language in Duclos' letter paralleled IMC's lobbying submissions:
- Concerns about "innovation" and "investment in Canada"
- Claims that the reforms would reduce pharmaceutical R&D
- Arguments that the comparator country basket was inappropriate

#### 5. The Result

The PMPRB complied with Minister Duclos' direction. The reforms were:
- Suspended before taking effect
- Effectively gutted
- Never implemented in their intended form

Pharmaceutical companies continued charging some of the highest prices in the world for prescription drugs in Canada.

#### 6. The Conflict

Minister Duclos used his position as Minister of Health to:
- Direct the PMPRB (an independent quasi-judicial body) to change its course of action
- Influence a decision that directly benefited IMC member companies
- Further the private financial interests of pharmaceutical corporations

---

### ANALYSIS: HOW THIS CONTRAVENES SECTION 9

#### Element 1: Use of Position as Public Office Holder

Minister Duclos held the position of Minister of Health, which gave him authority over health policy and direct influence over the PMPRB.

**Evidence:** His official ministerial letterhead, his public statements, and his statutory authority under the *Patent Act* and related regulations.

#### Element 2: Seeking to Influence a Decision of Another Person

Minister Duclos wrote directly to the Chair of the PMPRB — an independent board — directing it to suspend reforms. This was not a policy discussion; it was a ministerial directive to a quasi-judicial body.

**Evidence:** The letter itself (obtainable via ATIP or already disclosed), the PMPRB's subsequent compliance, and public statements by both parties.

#### Element 3: To Improperly Further Another Person's Private Interests

The decision to suspend drug price reforms directly and exclusively benefited:
- IMC member companies (Pfizer, J&J, AstraZeneca, Merck, etc.)
- These companies' private financial interests in maintaining high drug prices
- Estimated financial benefit: hundreds of millions of dollars annually in preserved profit margins

**Precedent:** In the *Trudeau II Report* (2019), Commissioner Mario Dion found that Prime Minister Trudeau contravened section 9 by using his position to influence the Attorney General's decision regarding SNC-Lavalin's criminal prosecution. The Commissioner reasoned:

> "Section 9 of the Act states that public office holders are prohibited from using their position to seek to influence a decision to improperly further the private interests of a third party."

> "SNC-Lavalin's considerable private financial interests would undoubtedly have been furthered had Mr. Trudeau successfully influenced the Attorney General."

The factual pattern here is analogous:
- Trudeau used his position to influence the Attorney General → Duclos used his position to influence the PMPRB
- SNC-Lavalin's private financial interests would be furthered → IMC member companies' private financial interests were furthered
- Both involved using ministerial authority to direct an independent body's decision-making

### ADDITIONAL CONCERNS

The complainant also notes the following matters for the Commissioner's attention:

1. **The Revolving Door:** Several IMC officials are former senior Health Canada or government officials. This creates an appearance of impropriety even if no specific contravention is proven.

2. **Paid Consultancy ($161,000 to StatCan):** IMC paid Statistics Canada for reports that were then used in lobbying. This raises questions about whether government-generated research was weaponized for private lobbying purposes.

3. **Pattern of Conduct:** The suspension of drug price reforms is part of a broader pattern where:
   - Canada has no universal pharmacare program (unlike comparable countries)
   - Pharmaceutical lobbying has consistently blocked or diluted pricing reforms
   - Ministerial interventions have aligned with industry interests
