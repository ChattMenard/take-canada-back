## CATEGORY A: Conflict of Interest / Political Corruption

### CASE 1: Trudeau II Report — SNC-Lavalin (2019)

**Citation:** Office of the Conflict of Interest and Ethics Commissioner, *Trudeau II Report* (August 2019)
**Key Finding:** Prime Minister Justin Trudeau contravened section 9 of the *Conflict of Interest Act*

**Relevant to Our Investigation:**
- Establishes that government officials using their position to influence decisions to further private interests is a **breach of federal law**
- The Ethics Commissioner found Trudeau sought to influence the Attorney General to intervene in SNC-Lavalin's criminal prosecution
- Commissioner Dion's reasoning on "private interests" and "public office" directly applicable to pharma lobbying influence on Health Minister Duclos

**Key Legal Principle:**
> "Section 9 of the Act states that public office holders are prohibited from using their position to seek to influence a decision to improperly further the private interests of a third party."

**How It Applies:**
- Health Minister Duclos intervened to suspend drug price reforms after pharma lobbying
- Duclos' letter was described as "indistinguishable from pharmaceutical lobby talking points"
- This parallels Trudeau's use of position to influence a decision for SNC-Lavalin's benefit
- **Arguably a section 9 breach** if the Ethics Commissioner were to investigate

**Source:** https://ciec-ccie.parl.gc.ca/en/investigations-enquetes/Pages/TrudeauIIReport-RapportTrudeauII.aspx

### CASE 2: R. v. Carson, 2018 SCC 37506

**Citation:** [2018] 2 SCR 239, 2018 SCC 25
**Charge:** Influence peddling (s. 121(1)(d) Criminal Code)
**Outcome:** **Conviction upheld by Supreme Court of Canada**

**Facts:**
- Bruce Carson, former Senior Advisor in the Prime Minister's Office, agreed to use government contacts to help H2O Professionals sell water treatment systems to First Nations
- In exchange, H2O promised to pay a commission to Carson's girlfriend
- Carson spoke to government officials to promote H2O's products

**Key Legal Principle (SCC Majority):**
> "Business relates to the government if it **depends on or could be facilitated by the government**, even if it requires government to change its operations to promote a specific result."
> "A broad interpretation is necessary because **real or apparent corruption may undermine the integrity and transparency that are crucial to democracy**."

**How It Applies:**
- IMC (pharma lobby) had 150+ meetings with government officials
- IMC successfully influenced Minister Duclos to suspend drug price reforms
- IMC paid StatCan $161K for reports used in lobbying
- The revolving door (Fralick: Health Canada → IMC; Hamelin: NSERC → IMC; Digby: pharma IP lawyer → PMPRB chair) constitutes influence peddling through institutional capture
- **Could be argued as s. 121(1)(d) influence peddling** — using government position or connections to facilitate business for a third party

**Source:** https://www.scc-csc.ca/judgments-jugements/cb/2018/37506/

### CASE 3: R. v. Grewal, 2023 ONSC 1516

**Citation:** 2023 ONSC 1516
**Charge:** Breach of trust (s. 122 Criminal Code)
**Outcome:** **Acquitted** (directed verdict)

**Facts:**
- MP Raj Grewal solicited loans from individuals to whom he had provided immigration assistance
- Crown alleged this was "in connection with the duties of his office"
- Defence argued the loans were from friends/family in the community

**Key Legal Principle (Even though acquitted, the framework is relevant):**
The Court set out the elements of breach of trust:
1. Accused is a public official
2. Accused was acting in connection with the duties of their office
3. Conduct represented a marked departure from the standard expected
4. Accused acted with intent to use public office for a purpose other than the public good

**The Judge's Finding:**
> "The Crown's case wasn't persuasive... There is no evidence to support an inference of guilt."
> "The inferences required to support the Crown's theory could neither reasonably nor logically be drawn."

**How It Applies:**
- The **legal framework** for breach of trust is established
- The standard is **high**: must prove intent to use office for corrupt purpose
- Difficulty of proving corrupt intent in political cases is demonstrated
- **Takeaway:** For accountability, focus on **documented actions** (like the Trudeau II Report or ATIP evidence of Duclos intervention) rather than intent

**Source:** https://www.minicounsel.ca/scj/2023/1516

## CATEGORY B: Transparency / Access to Information

### CASE 4: Canada (Information Commissioner) v. Canada (Minister of National Defence), 2011 SCC 25

**Citation:** [2011] 2 SCR 306, 2011 SCC 25
**Issue:** Whether ministers' records in ministerial offices are under the control of a government institution for ATIP purposes
**Outcome:** Records in ministerial offices **not** under institutional control

**Key Legal Principle:**
> "In the circumstances in which the records at issue in the first three applications were created and managed, a government institution would not have a reasonable expectation of obtaining them. These documents were therefore not under the control of a government institution."

**How It Applies:**
- This is a **barrier** to ATIP requests seeking ministerial records
- Records in ministerial offices (e.g., Duclos' intervention letter) may be outside ATIP scope
- However, once records are transferred to a department (e.g., Health Canada), they become "under control"
- **Strategy:** Target ATIP requests at **departments**, not ministers' offices, to capture documents that have been transferred

**Source:** https://www.canlii.org/en/ca/scc/doc/2011/2011scc25/2011scc25.html

### CASE 5: Merck Frosst Canada Ltd. v. Canada (Health), 2012 SCC 3

**Citation:** [2012] 1 SCR 23, 2012 SCC 3
**Issue:** Disclosure of third-party commercial information under ATIP
**Outcome:** Health Canada required to notify Merck before disclosure; certain commercial information exempt under s. 20(1)

**Key Legal Principle:**
> "The Act strikes a careful balance between the public interest in transparency and the need to protect confidential commercial information."

**How It Applies:**
- Pfizer/BioNTech contract information in Canada may be protected under s. 20(1) (third-party commercial information)
- But the **balance** can be challenged on public interest grounds
- The European leak of the Pfizer contract (via RAI) shows that this information IS available in other jurisdictions
- **Strategy:** Argue that if the EU can disclose the contract, Canada can too, and the public interest in vaccine transparency outweighs commercial confidentiality

**Source:** https://www.canlii.org/en/ca/scc/doc/2012/2012scc3/2012scc3.html

### CASE 6: Blank v. Canada (Minister of Justice), 2006 SCC 39

**Citation:** [2006] 2 SCR 319, 2006 SCC 39
**Issue:** Whether litigation privilege expires when litigation ends
**Outcome:** **Yes** — litigation privilege expires when the litigation that gave rise to it terminates

**Key Legal Principle:**
> "The Minister's claim of litigation privilege fails in this case because the privilege claimed, by whatever name, has expired: The files to which the respondent seeks access relate to penal proceedings that have long terminated."

**How It Applies:**
- If government claims privilege over pandemic-related documents, **privilege may have expired** if the litigation/pandemic "emergency" is over
- This is a tool to challenge privilege claims over COVID vaccine documents
- **Strategy:** Argue that any litigation privilege claimed over pandemic documents has expired because the "emergency" has ended

**Source:** https://www.canlii.org/en/ca/scc/doc/2006/2006scc39/2006scc39.html

## CATEGORY C: COVID Vaccine / Mandate Litigation

### CASE 7: Sakamoto v. Alberta (Proposed Class Action, 2024–present)

**Citation:** Alberta Court of King's Bench, Action No. 2401-05557
**Claims:** Negligent misrepresentation, negligence, misfeasance in public office, breach of fiduciary duty, conspiracy to commit assault and battery
**Status:** Pre-certification; defendants (federal and provincial governments) filed motions to strike

**Key Allegations:**
> "The Defendants misrepresented the Covid Vaccines, in breach of statutory duty, as 'safe and effective' to the public when in fact the Covid Vaccines were neither 'safe or effective.'"

> "The Defendants, acting through coordinated public messaging, mandates, and incentives, misrepresented material facts and implemented coercive measures that vitiated informed consent, exceeded statutory authority, and caused foreseeable harm."

**Key Evidence Cited:**
- Federal Defendant reported 488 deaths and 58,712 adverse events after COVID vaccination
- 11,702 listed as serious adverse events

**Government Defence (Canada):**
> "Canada does not owe a private law duty of care to individual recipients of a vaccine."
> "The proposed class is overbroad."

**How It Applies:**
- This is the **most directly applicable** ongoing litigation to our investigation
- The claims mirror our evidence: misrepresentation, lack of informed consent, coercion
- If certified, this class action could force disclosure of internal government communications
- **Strategy:** Monitor this case for document disclosure orders; evidence uncovered could strengthen our investigation
- The plaintiffs' evidence on adverse events (58,712+ reported) supports our Yale PVS study findings

**Source:** https://rathandcompany.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/02/2306-00442-FILED-2024-02-29-3rd-Amended-Statement-of-Claim.pdf

### CASE 8: VISP (Vaccine Injury Support Program) Failures

**Key Statistic (Global News Investigation, 2025):**
- PHAC and Oxaro initially predicted 40 VISP claims per year, then up to 400
- **More than 3,317 applications filed** as of June 2025
- 1,738 people still waiting for decisions
- **More than $16.5 million paid** to claimants
- 5 Canadians have launched lawsuits against governments and manufacturers

**Key Plaintiff:**
- Carrie Sakamoto (lead plaintiff in Alberta class action)
- Suffered severe Bell's Palsy after vaccination
- VISP granted her $62,500 but denied her request for reassessment
- VISP suggested her ailments were "unrelated" to the vaccine

**How It Applies:**
- Government's own compensation program acknowledges vaccine injuries exist
- The massive gap between predicted (400/year) and actual (3,317+) claims suggests the government underestimated the harm
- VISP's denial of claims mirrors FDA's concealment of adverse event data
- **Strategy:** VISP data is government-generated evidence of harm; ATIP requests for VISP claim numbers and denial rates would be powerful evidence

**Source:** https://globalnews.ca/news/11291443/visp-oxaro-lawsuits-carrie-sakamoto/

### CASE 9: Manitoba Charter Challenge (COVID Restrictions)

**Citation:** Manitoba Court of King's Bench
**Issue:** Whether COVID-19 public health restrictions violated Charter rights
**Outcome:** **Restrictions upheld** as reasonable limits under s. 1

**Key Principle:**
> "Interprovincial travel restrictions have been found constitutional; some members of the military failed to stop a mandatory vaccination policy; and quarantine hotels have been found sufficiently justifiable."

**How It Applies:**
- Shows courts have been **deferential** to government during pandemic
- Charter challenges to COVID measures have generally failed
- **Implication:** Civil litigation (torts, misrepresentation) may be more viable than Charter challenges
- Focus on **documented misrepresentation** rather than constitutional arguments

**Source:** https://www.cbc.ca/amp/1.6217925

## CATEGORY D: Pharmaceutical / Corporate Liability

### CASE 10: Opioid Class Actions (Koskie Minsky LLP)

**Citation:** Multiple class actions across Canada
**Claims:** Negligent misrepresentation, fraudulent misrepresentation, breach of Competition Act, breach of duty to warn
**Status:** Ongoing

**Key Principle:**
- Pharmaceutical manufacturers can be held liable for misrepresenting the safety of their products
- Duty to warn consumers about known risks
- Breach of Competition Act for anti-competitive practices

**How It Applies:**
- Pfizer/BioNTech's concealment of ingredients, adverse events, and manufacturing process parallels opioid manufacturers' conduct
- The FDA redactions and 75-year timeline mirror opioid manufacturers' document concealment strategies
- **Strategy:** Koskie Minsky's opioid litigation framework (misrepresentation + duty to warn + Competition Act) could be adapted for vaccine litigation

**Source:** https://kmlaw.ca/cases/liability-for-opioid-epidemic/

## CATEGORY E: U.S. Precedents (Relevant for Cross-Border Accountability)

### CASE 12: PHMPT v. FDA (N.D. Texas)

**Citation:** Public Health and Medical Professionals for Transparency v. FDA, Case No. 353278 (N.D. Texas)
**Issue:** FDA's refusal to release Pfizer vaccine data under FOIA
**Outcome:** Court ordered 55,000 pages/month; December 2024 order required EUA file production

**Key Rulings:**
- Judge Pittman (Jan 2022): "The FDA shall produce the remaining documents at a rate of 55,000 pages every 30 days"
- Judge (Dec 2024): "The Covid-19 pandemic is long passed and so has any legitimate reason for concealing from the American people the information relied upon by the government in approving the Pfizer Vaccine."

**How It Applies:**
- Directly applicable to Canadian ATIP requests for vaccine data
- U.S. court found **no legitimate reason** for concealing approval data once pandemic passed
- **Strategy:** Cite this case in Canadian ATIP challenges; argue that if U.S. courts order disclosure, Canadian courts should too

**Source:** https://storage.courtlistener.com/recap/gov.uscourts.txnd.353278/gov.uscourts.txnd.353278.24.0.pdf
