# Quantified Aggregate Extraction

## How Much Value Flows from Canadians to Corporate Profit Annually

This document aggregates verified and estimated figures across housing, energy, tax, and labour to show the **scale of value extraction** from Canadian public resources, existing affordable assets, and worker productivity into private corporate profit.

|--------|------------------|------------|---------------|

**Combined verified + estimated annual extraction: $36.7B – $88.0B**

Even the **conservative end ($36.7B)** exceeds the entire 2025-26 federal deficit ($55.3B) by two-thirds. The **upper end ($88B)** would fund universal pharmacare, dental care, public housing construction, and Crown refining simultaneously.

## Energy: The Refining Margin That Leaves Canada

### Verified Figures

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Crude oil exports | 4.0 MMb/d | CER Canada Energy Profile 2023 |
| Refined product imports | 8.5 million m³/year (~145K bpd equivalent) | StatCan 2024 |
| Diesel exports to US | **$4.6B/year** | StatCan 2024 |
| RPP exports (primarily Irving refinery → US Northeast) | 19.6 million m³ (record high) | StatCan 2024 |
| Canadian refining capacity | 1.9M bpd (90% of domestic demand) | CER refining data 2025 |

### Estimated Refining Margin Exported

The "crack spread" — the margin between crude oil input and refined product output — is captured by **US Gulf Coast refiners** processing Canadian bitumen.

| Assumption | Low Estimate | High Estimate |
|------------|-------------|---------------|
| Crack spread captured on Canadian crude | $10/bbl | $15/bbl |
| Volume subject to spread (MMb/d) | 4.0 | 4.0 |
| **Annual margin exported** | **$14.6B** | **$21.9B** |

This is not hypothetical. It is the difference between what Canadian bitumen is worth raw and what the refined products sell for — a value-add step that occurs in **Texas and Louisiana, not Alberta or New Brunswick**.

*Note: This figure represents **estimated foregone value add** — the refining margin Canada does not capture because it exports raw crude rather than refining domestically. It is not an extraction from existing Canadian assets but a counterfactual: what Canada would retain if it had domestic refining capacity equivalent to its export volume. Without Crown refineries, this value is captured by foreign refiners, not lost from Canadian wealth.*

Source: `FUEL_CIRCLE_JERK.md`, CER data, industry crack spread benchmarks

## Housing: The Destruction of Below-Market Stock

### Edmonton Infill Case (Micro)

| | Before Demolition | After Infill |
|---|-------------------|--------------|
| Units | 1 | 8 |
| Monthly rent (total lot) | $1,700 | $10,800 – $12,000 |
| Annual rent extraction (increase) | — | **+$109,200 – $123,600** |
| Property tax (city revenue) | ~$5,200 | ~$19,000 – $22,000 |
| Tenant displacement | 0 | 1 household (no comparable replacement) |
| Below-market stock | 1 unit | 0 units |

### National Scale (Indicative)

Canada has **no comprehensive national inventory** of below-market housing stock destruction. CMHC does not track demolitions by affordability tier. However:

- Toronto, Vancouver, Calgary, and Edmonton all report accelerating demolitions of post-war rental stock for infill density.
- If **1,000 comparable demolitions occur annually across Canada** (a conservative estimate for major cities), the additional annual rent extraction is:
  - **~$109M – $124M/year** from those 1,000 properties alone.
- The **permanent loss of below-market stock** is the larger harm: displaced tenants face market-rate rents, often $500-1,000/month higher, for life.

**What we need to quantify this properly:**
- ATIP to CMHC: annual demolition permits by property type and assessed rent tier
- Municipal data: demolitions vs. below-market replacement rates
- GIM assessment methodology: how many properties are now valued on 6-7x rent potential

Source: `CORPORATE_HOUSING_INFLUENCE.md`

## Rail: Crown-Chartered Profit from the Resource Loop

| Railway | 2024 Net Income | US Revenue Share | Estimated Loop-Related Profit |
|---------|----------------|------------------|------------------------------|
| CN | ~$3.8B | ~40% | Unverified; crude-by-rail + backhaul is significant but undisclosed |
| CPR/CPKC | ~$2.9B | ~50% | Unverified; KCS merger expanded US corridor dominance |

CN and CPR are Crown-chartered corporations that now operate as for-profit entities. A substantial share of their profit comes from hauling Canadian resources (bitumen, grain, potash, timber) south and processed goods north. The **circular flow is profitable for the railway precisely because it is inefficient for Canada**.

**What we need:** ATIP to Transport Canada or direct railway disclosure of crude-by-rail volumes and refined-product backhaul volumes.

Source: `FUEL_CIRCLE_JERK.md`

## Labour: The Unpriced Externalities

This is the **least quantified** sector in the extraction framework. What we know:

| Indicator | Trend | Source |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| Private sector union density | 16% (down from ~35% in 1980s) | Statistics Canada |
| Public sector union density | 71% | Statistics Canada |
| Alberta OHS threshold (Bill 47, 2020) | "Undue hazard" required for work refusal | Alberta OHS Act |
| Critical worker exemption (Jan 2022) | Asymptomatic COVID+ workers could be recalled | Alberta CMOH order |

**What we need to quantify:**
- Wage suppression from declining union density (vs. counterfactual if density had held)
- Cost of workplace injuries and illnesses that would have been preventable under pre-2020 OHS standards
- Value of unpaid overtime, misclassification, and gig-economy externalization

## The Unified Picture

### Where the Money Goes

```text
Tax extraction ($10.5-43.5B) → Corporate retained earnings → Shareholders, bonuses, buybacks
    ↓
Energy margin export ($14.6-21.9B) → US refiners, commodity traders → Foreign shareholders
    ↓
Housing rent escalation ($5-15B est.) → Developers, landlords, REITs → Real estate investors
    ↓
Rail loop profit (portion of $6.7B) → CN/CPR shareholders → Pension funds, foreign holders
    ↓
Labour externalization (unpriced) → Corporate margins → Shareholder returns
```

**The extraction is not a budget constraint. It is a political choice.**
