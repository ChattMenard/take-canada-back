## International Tax Context: Where Canada Actually Stands

### The Discrepancy: World Bank vs. OECD

A common claim circulating in this debate uses World Bank data showing Canada at **13.9% tax revenue as a share of GDP** versus Nordic countries at 40%+. This comparison is **methodologically invalid**.

| Data Source | Metric | Canada | Denmark | Finland | Norway | Sweden | OECD Avg |
|-------------|--------|--------|---------|---------|--------|--------|----------|
| **World Bank** `GC.TAX.TOTL.GD.ZS` | Central gov't tax revenue (% GDP) | **13.9%** (2024) | **33.4%** (2024) | **25.3%** (2024) | **25.4%** (2024) | **27.3%** (2024) | — |
| **OECD Revenue Statistics** | Total tax revenue, all gov't levels (% GDP) | **34.8%** (2023) | **44.0%** (2023) | **~42.8%** (2023) | **41.4%** (2023) | **~42.7%** (2023) | **33.9%** |

**Why the gap:** The World Bank indicator measures **central government tax revenue only**. Canada is a federal state where provinces collect significant taxes (income tax, sales tax, property tax). Nordic countries are unitary states where the central government collects most revenue. Comparing the World Bank figures across federal and unitary states is comparing apples to oranges.

The **OECD total tax revenue figure** is the only valid cross-country comparison because it includes all levels of government.

### The Real Position

- Canada collects **34.8% of GDP in total taxes** — barely above the OECD average of **33.9%** and **~7–10 percentage points below Nordic peers** (OECD, *Revenue Statistics 2025*).
- Canada ranked **20th out of 38 OECD countries** in tax-to-GDP ratio in 2023.
- Canada is **not a high-tax outlier**. It is a **moderate-tax jurisdiction** with room to increase revenue without exceeding Nordic levels.

### Corporate Tax Specifically

The argument for raising corporate taxes on the largest firms requires examining corporate tax specifically:

| Metric | Canada | Norway | Sweden | Denmark | Finland |
|--------|--------|--------|--------|---------|---------|
| Statutory combined corporate rate | ~26.5% (15% fed + ~11.5% prov) | 22% | 20.6% | 22% | 20% |
| Corporate tax revenue (% GDP) | Above OECD average | Above OECD average (energy sector) | Near OECD average | Near OECD average | Near OECD average |

Canada's **statutory corporate tax rate is already higher** than all four Nordic countries. However, Canada's **effective tax rate on large multinationals** is lower than the statutory rate due to:

- Accelerated depreciation and tax credits
- Profit shifting to lower-tax jurisdictions
- Thin capitalization rules that are weaker than Nordic equivalents
- The **income sprinkling** and **passive income** loopholes inside private corporations

The progressive corporate tax proposal does not raise Canada's statutory rate above Nordic levels. It closes the gap between **statutory rate** (what the law says) and **effective rate** (what large firms actually pay) by applying higher marginal rates on profits above $20M — the segment of the corporate sector that benefits most from base erosion.

### What This Means for the Flight Debate

If Canada raised its total tax revenue from 34.8% to Nordic levels (~42–44%), it would generate an additional **$180–250B annually** in total government revenue. The progressive corporate tax proposal captures only a fraction of that gap (**$10.5–43.5B**) — concentrated on the 7,250 largest firms that are least likely to flee because they are tethered to Canadian assets, consumers, and resources.

Canada is not proposing to out-tax the Nordics. It is proposing to **stop under-collecting** from its largest corporate taxpayers while remaining below Nordic total tax burdens.

## The Flight Scenarios

### Assumptions

- The **$43.5B** figure assumes all 7,250 corporations pay the new progressive rates.
- The **$10.5B** figure assumes only multinational/large foreign-controlled firms are affected.
- Corporations that leave cease contributing *all* Canadian corporate tax revenue from Canadian operations.
- Corporations that stay pay the new progressive rates.
- Domestic competitors or public alternatives capture market share from departures.

### Scenario A: No Flight (Best Case)

- **New revenue:** $43.5B annually
- **Deficit impact:** Eliminates 79% of the $55.3B deficit
- **Net position:** +$43.5B

### Scenario B: 50% Flight (Mid Case)

- **Corporations remaining:** ~3,625
- **Revenue from those who stay:** ~$21.75B (proportional)
- **Revenue lost from those who leave:** Their current 27% contribution
- **Net new revenue:** Still positive, because the ones who leave were already paying 27%; the ones who stay pay 30-37.5%.

If the departed 50% were contributing, say, $15B at the old 27% rate, the net is:
- Lost: $15B
- Gained from stayers: $21.75B (incremental over their old contribution)
- **Net new revenue: ~$6.75B+ annually**

Even with **half of them leaving**, the tax base is more productive.

### Scenario C: 90% Flight (Worst Credible Case)

- **Corporations remaining:** ~725
- **Revenue from those who stay:** ~$4.35B (proportional)
- **If the departed 90% were contributing $40B at old rates:**
  - Lost: $40B
  - Gained: $4.35B
  - **Net: -$35.65B**

This is the only scenario where flight causes net harm — and it requires **nine out of ten** of Canada's largest profit generators to abandon their Canadian operations entirely.

## Why 90% Flight Is Not Credible

| Barrier to Flight | Reason |
|-------------------|--------|
| **Sunk infrastructure** | These firms own Canadian assets, supply chains, and customer bases that cannot be relocated |
| **Market access** | Leaving Canada means forfeiting 40M consumers and resource access |
| **Replacement speed** | Domestic competitors or Crown entities can absorb market share within 12-24 months |
| **Global precedent** | Corporations threatened flight in Australia, UK, and France — most stayed and paid |
| **Profitability threshold** | A firm with $20M+ Canadian profits is not operating on 27% margins; a 10.5% rate increase does not erase profitability |

## The "Do It Ourselves" Alternative

If a corporation exits, its market function does not disappear. The options:

| Sector | Corporate Function | Domestic Alternative |
|--------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Banking | Mortgage lending, deposits | Credit unions, postal banking, Crown corporation expansion |
| Energy | Refining, distribution | Public ownership models, domestic mid-tier operators |
| Telecom | Connectivity | Municipal broadband, co-ops |
| Retail | Consumer goods | Co-op networks, regional chains |
| Housing | Development | Non-profit builders, CMHC direct development |

### Historical Precedent

- **Alberta Treasury Branches:** Provincial public bank operating since 1938 with $17B in assets.
- **SaskTel:** Crown telecom delivering comparable service at lower consumer cost.
- **Caisses populaires (Quebec):** Co-op credit unions with 4.5M members.
- **Municipal broadband (Chattanooga, TN):** Proved gigabit service can be delivered publicly at 1/3 the corporate price.

The only losing scenario requires the vast majority of affected firms to walk away from profitable Canadian operations. Even then, the lost functions are replaceable through domestic enterprise, public ownership, or smaller competitors who would not face the progressive rates.

### Reframing the Debate

The corporate lobby frames this as "Canada driving away investment." The reality:

- **Current model:** Canada collects 27% from corporations who then decide what gets built, where, and for whose profit.
- **Alternative model:** Canada collects 30-37.5% from the largest 7,250 firms and uses a fraction of that revenue to **directly own strategic infrastructure** that serves public interest rather than shareholder returns.

A Crown-owned pipeline does not need to maximize quarterly profit. A public refinery can price fuel at cost rather than margin. A Crown bank can offer mortgages at cost-plus rather than market rate.

This is not theory — it is how Alberta Treasury Branches, SaskTel, and Hydro-Québec already operate.

## Worst Case: We Buy What They Leave Behind

If a corporation exits, Canada does not simply lose the function — it gains the **opportunity to acquire the assets at fire-sale prices.**

### The Acquisition Playbook

A departing multinational cannot pack up a refinery, pipeline, or telecom network and move it to Delaware. The physical assets, leases, permits, and workforce stay in Canada. The government or a Crown corporation can purchase these assets for cents on the dollar from a seller with no leverage and no buyers.

| Asset Type | Typical Depreciation | Acquisition Discount | Public Benefit |
|------------|---------------------|---------------------|----------------|
| Refinery | 30-50% book value | 40-60% below replacement | Public fuel pricing |
| Pipeline ROW + infrastructure | 25-40% | 30-50% below replacement | Crown-controlled transport |
| Telecom fiber/cell towers | 40-60% | 50-70% below replacement | Universal public broadband |
| Banking branches + loan books | 20-35% | 25-45% below book | Postal banking expansion |
| Housing development land | Market-dependent | Distressed sale pricing | Public non-profit housing |

### The Economic Cascade

Public acquisition of departing corporate assets triggers a self-reinforcing economic cycle:

1. **Unemployment drops:** Crown corporations do not offshore. Workers keep their jobs under new ownership.
2. **Wages stay local:** Profits that previously flowed to foreign shareholders are retained as public revenue or reinvested as wages.
3. **Spending increases:** Local wages generate local demand — the economic multiplier effect.
4. **Dollars per capita rise:** Publicly owned enterprises price at cost-plus rather than profit-maximize, leaving disposable income in Canadian households.
5. **Tax base broadens:** Employed workers pay income tax. Spending generates sales tax. The economy self-boosts.

### The Union and Worker Safety Dimension

Public ownership changes the power dynamic. Crown corporations are not hostile to unionization — in many cases they are unionized by design. Public accountability means worker safety standards are subject to parliamentary oversight, not quarterly earnings pressure.

This matters because Canada has already demonstrated what happens when worker safety is subordinated to corporate convenience:

#### Alberta's OHS Rollback (Bill 47, 2020)

- **"Undue hazard" threshold:** Bill 47 amended the OHS Act so workers could only refuse work if it posed a "serious and immediate threat." A cumulative or potential risk — like COVID-19 exposure — no longer qualified.
- **Critical worker exemption (January 2022):** The Chief Medical Officer allowed employers to recall asymptomatic or mildly symptomatic COVID-positive employees. The employer decided who was "essential."
- **Result:** Workers who feared pandemic exposure had severely limited legal avenues to refuse unsafe work. The threshold was deliberately raised to keep corporate operations running at worker expense.

#### The Alternative

Public ownership restores the balance:

- **Parliamentary accountability:** Crown corporation safety practices are subject to committee review, ATIP requests, and ministerial oversight.
- **Union density:** Public sector unionization rates in Canada are roughly 3x higher than private sector (71% vs. 16%). Public acquisition tends to expand union coverage.
- **Global leadership:** Canada historically led the world in worker safety innovation. Public ownership removes the profit motive that erodes those standards.

### Reframing "Flight" as Opportunity

Corporate lobbyists threaten flight as a catastrophe. The honest framing:

- **Their threat:** "If you tax us, we will abandon our Canadian assets."
- **The reality:** "If you tax us, we will sell you our Canadian assets at a discount, and you will run them better."

A foreign-owned refinery optimizing for quarterly dividends will cut maintenance, defer safety upgrades, and resist unionization. A Crown-owned refinery optimizing for public benefit will maintain the plant, invest in worker safety, and bargain collectively.

The economy does not shrink when corporations leave — it **democratizes.**

## Bottom Line

**The corporate tax increase is a no-lose proposition.**

- If they stay: Canada collects $10.5-43.5B annually and eliminates most of the deficit.
- If some leave: Canada still nets positive revenue from those who remain, and the departed market share is absorbed domestically.
- If many leave: The break-even point requires near-total flight — an outcome with no historical precedent in comparable jurisdictions.

The threat is a negotiating tactic. The math says call the bluff.
