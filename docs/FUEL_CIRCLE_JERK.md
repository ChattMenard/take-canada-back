# The Canadian Fuel Circle Jerk

## How Canada Extracts Oil, Ships It South, and Buys It Back at a Markup

---

## The Loop

| Step | What Happens | Who Profits | Who Loses |
|------|--------------|-------------|-----------|
| 1. Extract | Heavy bitumen mined from Athabasca oil sands | Foreign oil majors, royalty collectors | Canadian workers (jobs limited to extraction) |
| 2. Pipe south | Bitumen diluted and piped via Keystone / Enbridge to US Gulf Coast | Pipeline operators (tolls) | Canadian sovereignty (resource leaves) |
| 3. Refine in Texas | US refineries configured for heavy sour crude process Canadian bitumen | US refiners (Marathon, Valero, Exxon) — crack spread margin | Canadian refiners (no heavy-crude capacity) |
| 4. Sell at global price | Refined products (gasoline, diesel, jet) sold on global market | US refiners, commodity traders | Canadian consumers (pay global price regardless) |
| 5. Import back north | Refined products shipped back to Canada, especially Atlantic Canada | Transport operators, middlemen | Canadian consumers (pay transport + margin + exchange rate) |

---

## The Irving Refinery Twist

The Irving refinery in Saint John, New Brunswick — the **only refinery east of Quebec** — adds a second layer of absurdity:

- **Does not process Canadian crude.** Irving is configured for light sweet crude, not heavy Canadian bitumen. It imports from Saudi Arabia, Nigeria, and other foreign sources.
- **Exports ~80% of its refined product** to the US Northeast (New York, Boston) because those markets pay higher prices.
- **Atlantic Canada imports from the US Gulf Coast** instead — the same region processing Canadian bitumen.

So Atlantic Canada gets:
- **No Canadian bitumen** (it went to Texas)
- **No Irving product** (it went to New York)
- **US Gulf Coast imports** at global price + marine transport + margin

**The same gallon of gasoline touches three countries to get from Alberta to Nova Scotia.**

## The Railway Middlemen

CN (Canadian National) and CPR/CPKC (Canadian Pacific Kansas City) are not passive infrastructure — they are active profiteers from the same loop:

- **Crude-by-rail:** When pipelines hit capacity or face delays, CN and CPR haul bitumen south in tank cars at premium rates. During the Keystone XL delays and Line 3 shutdowns, crude-by-rail volumes spiked.
- **Refined products north:** The same rail corridors haul refined products (gasoline, diesel) back to Canadian terminals — often the same tank cars, backhauled empty otherwise.
- **Coal, potash, grain:** The same pattern applies across Canadian resources — extract, rail to port, export raw, buy back processed.

CN and CPR are Crown-chartered corporations (CPR via the original Dominion charter, CN via the 1919 nationalization) that now operate as for-profit entities prioritizing shareholder returns over Canadian sovereignty. Both report billions in annual profit. Both would fall squarely into the $20M+ profit bracket under the proposed progressive corporate tax.

| Railway | 2024 Net Income | Canadian HQ | US Market Exposure |
|---------|----------------|-------------|-------------------|
| CN | ~$3.8B | Montreal | ~40% revenue from US |
| CPR/CPKC | ~$2.9B | Calgary | ~50% revenue from US |

## Who Benefits

| Stakeholder | Benefit |
|-------------|---------|
| US Gulf Coast refiners | Cheap Canadian heavy feedstock + global product price = maximum crack spread |
| Pipeline operators | Tolls on southbound bitumen; potential tolls on northbound refined products |
| CN / CPR | Premium crude-by-rail rates; backhaul refined product contracts |
| Irving Oil | Arbitrage: buy cheap foreign light crude, sell refined product to premium US Northeast market |
| Commodity traders | Price differentials between WCS (Western Canadian Select), WTI, and global benchmarks |
| Foreign oil majors | Extract Canadian resource, pay minimal royalty, book profit abroad |

---

## Who Loses

| Stakeholder | Loss |
|-------------|------|
| Canadian consumers | Pay global price at the pump regardless of domestic crude origin |
| Atlantic Canada | Highest fuel prices in Canada; no local refining of Canadian crude; dependent on imports |
| Canadian workers | Refining jobs in Texas and Louisiana instead of Alberta, Ontario, or Atlantic Canada |
| Canadian sovereignty | No control over domestic fuel supply; vulnerable to US refinery outages, export restrictions, or geopolitical disruption |
| Canadian treasury | Royalties on raw extraction are a fraction of the value captured by US refining |

## The Alternative

**Crown-owned domestic refining** changes the entire equation:

- **Alberta:** Build or acquire heavy-crude upgraders and refineries. Process Canadian bitumen into Canadian refined product.
- **Ontario/Quebec:** Expand Sarnia and Montreal corridor refining for domestic distribution.
- **Atlantic Canada:** Crown-owned refinery processing Canadian crude for Atlantic Canadian consumers — not for export to New York.

| Current Model | Crown Refining Model |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Bitumen → Texas → Refined product → Atlantic Canada | Bitumen → Crown refinery → Atlantic Canada |
| Margin to US refiners | Margin to Canadian Crown corporation |
| Jobs in Texas | Jobs in Alberta, Ontario, New Brunswick |
| Price set by global market + transport | Price set by cost-plus public mandate |
| Vulnerable to US export restrictions | Sovereign control of domestic supply |

---

## Bottom Line

Canada is the only major oil producer that does not control its own refined product supply chain from wellhead to pump. We extract the raw material, give it away to US refiners, and buy back the finished product at a markup.

The $8.5B investment in domestic refining capacity (per `TRANSPARENCY_CAMPAIGN.md`) is not an expense. It is the cost of **stopping the circle jerk**.
